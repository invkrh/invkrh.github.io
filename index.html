Before demonstrating the equation $\eqref{eq2}$, let’s review the properties of positive definite symmetric matrix.

Positive definite symmetric matrix

A real symmetric $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is called positive definite if $x^TAx>0$ for all non-zero vectors $x$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

  1. Prove that the eigenvalues of A are all positive.
  2. Prove that AAA is invertible.
  3. Prove that A−1A^{−1}A−1 is symmetric.
  4. Prove that A−1A^{−1}A−1 is positive-definite.

Proof 1: the eigenvalues of AAA are all positive

Let $\lambda$ be a (real) eigenvalue of $A$ and let $x$ be a corresponding real eigenvector. That is, we have

Ax=λxAx=\lambda x Ax=λx

Then we multiply by $x^T$ on left and obtain,

xTAx=λxTx=λ∥x∥2\begin{aligned} x^T A x &=\lambda x^T x \\ &=\lambda\|x\|^{2} \end{aligned} xTAx​=λxTx=λ∥x∥2​

The left hand side is positive as $A$ is positive definite and $x$ is a nonzero vector as it is an eigenvector.
Since the norm $\|x\|^2$ is positive, we must have $\lambda$ is positive.
It follows that every eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $A$ is real.

Proof 2: AAA is invertible

Since proof 1, the matrix $A$ does not have 0 as an eigenvalue

We can prove this by contradiction:
If $Ax=0 \cdot x$ for some $x \neq 0$ then by definition of eigenvalues (non-invertible), $x$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda = 0$

Alternatively, we can prove this by using determinent

det⁡A=∏inλi>0\operatorname{det} A = \prod_i^n \lambda_i \gt 0 detA=i∏n​λi​>0

Proof c: A−1A^{−1}A−1 is symmetric

Let $A$ be the invertible symmetric matrix, then $AA^{-1} = I$ and $A = A^T$.

Since $I = I^T$,

AA−1=(AA−1)TA A^{-1}=\left(A A^{-1}\right)^{T} AA−1=(AA−1)T

Since $(A B)^{T}=B^{T} A^{T}$,

AA−1=(A−1)TATA A^{-1}=\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} A^{T} AA−1=(A−1)TAT

Since $A A^{-1}=A^{-1} A=I$, we rearrange the left side,

A−1A=(A−1)TATA^{-1} A=\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} A^{T} A−1A=(A−1)TAT

Since $A = A^T$, we substitute the right side,

A−1A=(A−1)TAA−1A(A−1)=(A−1)TA(A−1)A−1I=(A−1)TIA−1=(A−1)T\begin{aligned} A^{-1} A &=\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} A \\ A^{-1} A\left(A^{-1}\right) &=\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} A\left(A^{-1}\right) \\ A^{-1} I &=\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} I \\ A^{-1} &=\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} \end{aligned} A−1AA−1A(A−1)A−1IA−1​=(A−1)TA=(A−1)TA(A−1)=(A−1)TI=(A−1)T​

Q.E.D.

Proof 2

Knowing that $A$ is an invertible symmetric matrix, then $\dfrac{\partial \log \operatorname{det}(A)}{\partial A} = A^{-1}$

Since the chain rule,

∂log⁡det⁡(A)∂A=1det⁡(A)∂det⁡(A)∂A\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial \log \operatorname{det}(A)}{\partial A} &= \frac{1}{\operatorname{det}(A)} \frac{\partial \operatorname{det}(A)}{\partial A} \end{aligned} ∂A∂logdet(A)​​=det(A)1​∂A∂det(A)​​

According to the cookbook: $\dfrac{\partial \operatorname{det}(A)}{\partial A}=\operatorname{det}(A)\left(A^{-1}\right)^{T}$,

∂log⁡det⁡(A)∂A=(A−1)T\frac{\partial \log \operatorname{det}(A)}{\partial A} = \left(A^{-1}\right)^{T} ∂A∂logdet(A)​=(A−1)T

Since the proof 1,

∂log⁡det⁡(A)∂A=A−1\frac{\partial \log \operatorname{det}(A)}{\partial A} = A^{-1} ∂A∂logdet(A)​=A−1

Q.E.D.
